Lucille Tenazas Early Career

Lucille Tenazas is a Filipino-born graphic designer, educator, and design leader whose influential career has shaped both visual communication and design education for more than four decades. Born in Manila in 1953, she showed early artistic talent and studied at the College of the Holy Spirit before moving to the United States to pursue postgraduate design education, earning an MFA in design from the Cranbrook Academy of Art. In 1982, she founded Tenazas Design, working first in New York and later in San Francisco before returning to New York. Her studio collaborated with major cultural and educational institutions including the San Francisco Museum of Modern Art, Stanford University Art Museum, UC Berkeley, and Chronicle Books. Her work places typography and linguistic meaning at the center of design, exploring how language and form interact to create layered and poetic visual experiences influenced by modernist movements such as Russian Constructivism and De Stijl.

Achievements and Impact

Beyond professional practice, Tenazas has had a major impact as an educator and design leader. She served as the Founding Chair of the MFA Design Program at California College of the Arts, where she emphasized form-giving, leadership, and thoughtful creative development. She later became the Henry Wolf Professor of Communication Design at Parsons School of Design, where she continues mentoring emerging designers. Tenazas also contributed significantly through AIGA, serving as national president from 1996 to 1998. She received the AIGA Medal in 2013 and was named a 2019 AIGA San Francisco Fellow, recognizing her lasting contributions to design and education. Her work is held in the permanent collection of SFMOMA.

Continuing Influence

Today, Tenazas remains a vital figure in contemporary graphic design, respected as both a practitioner and mentor. Through ongoing teaching, leadership, and continued exploration of typography and language-driven design, she continues to influence future generations while expanding conversations around critical design practice and the cultural role of design.
